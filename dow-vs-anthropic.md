# Anthropic vs. the Pentagon: how an AI safety standoff became a constitutional crisis

**Anthropic is the only major AI company to refuse the Trump administration's demand for unrestricted military AI access—and paid for it with a federal ban and an unprecedented "supply chain risk" designation.** The February 2026 confrontation between the $380 billion AI startup and the Department of War (formerly Defense) represents the most consequential clash between a technology company and the U.S. government since the Apple-FBI encryption fight of 2016, but with far higher stakes. At its core, the dispute centers on two narrow "red lines" Anthropic refused to cross: no mass domestic surveillance of Americans, and no fully autonomous weapons. The confrontation escalated from months of private negotiation to a 72-hour public crisis that drew in the President, Congress, rival AI companies, and thousands of tech workers—and remains unresolved as Anthropic prepares to challenge the government's actions in court.

## From partner to pariah: a timeline of escalating conflict

The Anthropic-Pentagon relationship didn't begin as adversarial. In **November 2024**, Anthropic partnered with Palantir Technologies and AWS to deploy Claude on classified government networks—making it **the first frontier AI model cleared for classified military use**. By **July 2025**, the Department of War's Chief Digital and Artificial Intelligence Office awarded Anthropic a **$200 million** prototype contract alongside Google, OpenAI, and xAI. Anthropic built custom "Claude Gov" models for national security customers and offered Claude to federal agencies for $1 through a GSA partnership. The company's acceptable use policy permitted intelligence analysis and military planning while prohibiting specific categories of harm—a nuanced approach that avoided the blanket military bans some competitors had originally adopted.

The first public crack appeared on **October 14, 2025**, when White House AI & Crypto Czar **David Sacks** attacked Anthropic co-founder Jack Clark on X, accusing the company of "running a sophisticated regulatory capture strategy based on fear-mongering" and trying to "backdoor Woke AI" through state legislation. A week later, CEO **Dario Amodei** published a formal rebuttal, listing Anthropic's defense credentials and insisting that "managing the societal impacts of AI should be a matter of policy over politics." Behind the scenes, friction had been building: Anthropic had **lobbied against a 10-year moratorium on state AI laws** in Trump's "One Big Beautiful Bill" (the moratorium later failed 99–1 in the Senate), and had **declined requests from federal law enforcement contractors** seeking to use Claude for surveillance of U.S. citizens.

The rupture accelerated in **January 2026** after U.S. special operations forces captured Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro in a raid on Caracas. Claude was reportedly used during the operation through Anthropic's Palantir partnership—the first confirmed use of a commercial frontier AI model in a classified military operation. When a senior Anthropic executive contacted Palantir to ask whether Claude had been used, the exchange was reported back to the Pentagon, which interpreted it as Anthropic potentially disapproving of the operation. Anthropic denied this characterization, but the damage was done.

On **January 9, 2026**, Defense Secretary **Pete Hegseth** released an "AI Acceleration Strategy" memo declaring the Pentagon would become an "AI-first warfighting force" and mandating models "free from usage policy constraints that may limit lawful military applications." Three days later, at a SpaceX facility, Hegseth announced: "We will not employ AI models that won't allow you to fight wars... Department of War AI will not be woke. It will work for us."

## The 72-hour showdown that split Silicon Valley

The crisis reached its climax in the final week of February 2026. On **Tuesday, February 24**, Hegseth summoned Amodei to the Pentagon for a tense meeting attended by Deputy Secretary Steve Feinberg, Under Secretary Emil Michael, and Pentagon general counsel Earl Matthews. Hegseth delivered an ultimatum: agree to unrestricted "all lawful purposes" use by **5:01 PM ET Friday, February 27**, or face contract termination, a supply chain risk designation, and potential invocation of the **Defense Production Act**—a Korean War-era law that could compel compliance.

On **Thursday, February 26**, the Pentagon sent new contract language overnight. Anthropic responded that it "made virtually no progress on preventing Claude's use for mass surveillance of Americans or in fully autonomous weapons" and that "new language framed as compromise was paired with legalese that would allow those safeguards to be disregarded at will." Pentagon spokesman **Sean Parnell** posted the deadline publicly on X. Under Secretary **Emil Michael** called Amodei "a liar" with "a God-complex" who "wants nothing more than to try to personally control the US Military."

Amodei published his own statement that day: **"We cannot in good conscience accede to their request."** He argued that AI systems are "simply not reliable enough" for fully autonomous weapons and that mass domestic surveillance is "incompatible with democratic values," while emphasizing he "believe[s] deeply in the existential importance of using AI to defend the United States."

When the Friday deadline passed, **Trump posted on Truth Social**: "The Leftwing nut jobs at Anthropic have made a DISASTROUS MISTAKE trying to STRONG-ARM the Department of War." He directed "EVERY Federal Agency in the United States Government to IMMEDIATELY CEASE all use of Anthropic's technology," threatened "major civil and criminal consequences," and called Anthropic a "radical left, woke company." Ninety minutes later, Hegseth designated Anthropic a **"Supply-Chain Risk to National Security"**—a classification previously reserved for foreign adversaries like Huawei—barring any Pentagon contractor from conducting commercial activity with the company. Elon Musk added on X: "Anthropic hates Western Civilization."

In an extraordinary twist, **OpenAI CEO Sam Altman** announced that same evening that OpenAI had reached its own classified-network agreement with the Pentagon—one that included **the exact same two red lines** Anthropic had demanded: "prohibitions on domestic mass surveillance and human responsibility for the use of force, including for autonomous weapon systems." Altman stated: "The DoW agrees with these principles, reflects them in law and policy, and we put them into our agreement."

## Anthropic's ethical framework and what the red lines actually mean

Anthropic's positions are rooted in its **Responsible Scaling Policy** (RSP), first published in September 2023 and modeled on biosafety levels. The RSP established AI Safety Levels (ASLs) with escalating safeguards tied to model capabilities. The original version committed Anthropic to **pausing model training** if safety measures couldn't keep pace—a commitment that drew industry attention and admiration from AI safety advocates.

However, **RSP Version 3.0**, released on **February 24, 2026**—the same day as the Hegseth ultimatum—**dropped the hard commitment to pause training**. Chief Science Officer **Jared Kaplan** told TIME: "We didn't really feel, with the rapid advance of AI, that it made sense for us to make unilateral commitments… if competitors are blazing ahead." The new policy requires both that Anthropic leads the AI race and that material catastrophic risk exists before any pause would be considered. Anthropic cited "an anti-regulatory political climate" as context for the revision, though a spokesperson told the Wall Street Journal the timing was coincidental to the Pentagon negotiations. Independent reviewer Chris Painter of METR warned that society is "not prepared for catastrophic risks."

The company's **Constitutional AI** approach trains Claude using explicit principles drawn from the UN Declaration of Human Rights and other frameworks. Through a "Collective Constitutional AI" experiment, roughly 1,000 Americans voted on model principles, generating **38,252 votes** on 1,127 statements—finding high consensus on most issues but disagreement on topics like diversity principles.

Anthropic's two Pentagon red lines are notably **narrow rather than sweeping**. They don't prohibit foreign intelligence surveillance, targeted killing with human oversight, offensive military operations, or most other defense applications. In a CBS interview on February 28, Amodei clarified he is "not categorically against fully autonomous weapons" but believes "AI's reliability isn't there yet," and noted that mass domestic surveillance "actually isn't illegal. It was just never useful before the era of AI. So there's this way in which domestic mass surveillance is getting ahead of the law." The company estimated it agrees with **98–99%** of military use cases.

## Every other AI company chose a different path

Anthropic's stance becomes sharper when measured against the industry. **OpenAI** has been the most politically accommodating: CEO Sam Altman appeared alongside Trump at the White House in January 2025 to announce the **$500 billion Stargate Project**; the company submitted policy proposals explicitly endorsing Trump's AI agenda and warning about China; co-founder **Greg Brockman donated $25 million to a Trump Super PAC** and backed a **$125 million+ bipartisan PAC** opposing state AI regulation. OpenAI had quietly removed its blanket ban on military use in January 2024, a full year before the Trump inauguration.

**Google** executed the most dramatic reversal. After the 2018 **Project Maven** employee revolt—where thousands of workers demanded Google exit the "business of war"—the company had pledged not to develop AI for weapons or surveillance. On **February 4, 2025**, Google **formally removed that pledge** from its AI Principles. DeepMind CEO Demis Hassabis framed the shift as necessary for democracies to lead AI development. Google accepted the Pentagon's "all lawful uses" framework.

**Meta** pursued deep personal courtship of Trump: Zuckerberg replaced liberal policy chief Nick Clegg with Republican Joel Kaplan, donated $1 million to the inauguration, ended third-party fact-checking, terminated DEI programs, and pledged **$600 billion in U.S. infrastructure investment**. Meta permitted military use of its open-source Llama models. **xAI** (Musk's company) agreed to unrestricted "all lawful purposes" military use and was the second company (after Anthropic) cleared for classified networks. Only **Microsoft** maintained a somewhat moderate public posture, with CEO Satya Nadella skipping Trump's inauguration, though the company quietly expanded defense and ICE work.

The irony of the Anthropic ban was not lost on observers: OpenAI secured a classified-network deal with the same safety provisions Anthropic had demanded, while Anthropic was punished for requesting them. Former Trump Senior Policy Advisor for AI **Dean Ball** called the government's action "simply attempted corporate murder" and said he "could not possibly recommend investing in American AI to any investor."

## The Trump administration built a deregulatory architecture piece by piece

The administration's posture toward AI safety has been consistent and escalating since inauguration day. On **January 20, 2025**, Trump revoked Biden's **Executive Order 14110** on AI safety—which had mandated red-teaming, safety reporting, and bias assessments. Three days later, he signed **EO 14179**, declaring it U.S. policy to "sustain and enhance America's global AI dominance" with systems "free from ideological bias or engineered social agendas."

The **AI Action Plan** released **July 23, 2025** contained 90 federal policy positions across three pillars: accelerating innovation, building infrastructure, and international security leadership. It directed NIST to **eliminate references to Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion** from its AI Risk Management Framework. The **December 11, 2025** executive order created an **AI Litigation Task Force** to challenge state AI laws and made states with restrictive legislation ineligible for federal broadband funding.

Key administration figures drove the anti-safety-regulation narrative. **David Sacks**, appointed AI & Crypto Czar on December 5, 2024, framed AI safety advocacy as "the doomer industrial complex." **Peter Thiel's** network permeated the administration—JD Vance (VP), Michael Kratsios (OSTP Director), and Sacks himself are all Thiel associates, while Thiel-backed defense companies Palantir and Anduril secured billions in contracts. Hegseth's January 2026 AI memo made the position explicit: "We must accept that the risks of not moving fast enough outweigh the risks of imperfect alignment."

## The deeper question neither side has answered

The Anthropic-Pentagon confrontation exposed a genuine policy vacuum. **Congress has not passed comprehensive AI legislation**, leaving fundamental questions—Should AI select military targets without human approval? Should AI enable mass surveillance of citizens?—unresolved by democratic process. As Amodei noted on 60 Minutes in November 2025: "I think I'm deeply uncomfortable with these decisions being made by a few companies, by a few people." Anderson Cooper asked the obvious follow-up: "Who elected you and Sam Altman?"

The support Anthropic received was strikingly bipartisan. Senator **Mark Warner** (D-VA) warned that national security decisions may be "driven by political considerations." Senators **Ed Markey** and **Chris Van Hollen** called the Pentagon's threats "a chilling abuse of government power." Eleven OpenAI employees and over 100 Google workers signed letters supporting Anthropic. But Anthropic also invested **$20 million** in Public First Action, a PAC backing pro-regulation candidates in 2026 midterms—a direct counter to the $125 million+ anti-regulation fund backed by OpenAI insiders and Andreessen Horowitz.

The standoff revealed a paradox at the heart of American AI policy: the company most willing to work with the military while maintaining safety guardrails was punished, while the company that secured identical guardrails through political accommodation was rewarded. Anthropic's $14 billion in annual revenue and $380 billion valuation provide financial resilience, but the supply chain risk designation—if upheld—could force partners like Amazon, which has invested $4 billion in Anthropic, to choose between their defense business and their AI investment. Anthropic has pledged to challenge the designation in court, setting up what may become the defining legal battle over the government's power to compel AI companies to remove safety restrictions from their products.

The confrontation is not, as the administration frames it, about whether AI should serve national defense. Anthropic agreed to 98–99% of military applications. It is about whether the remaining 1–2%—autonomous weapons and mass surveillance—represent lines that a democracy should want its AI companies to draw, or obstacles that warfighters cannot afford. That question, ultimately, belongs to Congress and the courts. As of March 2026, neither has weighed in.
